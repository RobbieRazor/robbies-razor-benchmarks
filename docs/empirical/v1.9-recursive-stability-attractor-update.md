# 1 Recursive Stability Attractor

Recursive systems rarely begin in stable compression regimes.

Instead they frequently evolve through alternating phases:

expansion
→ constraint accumulation
→ compression innovation
→ stability restoration
→ renewed expansion


Across repeated cycles the system approaches the **Stability Minimum** defined in §11.4.

Observed examples include:

| Domain | Expansion | Compression |
|------|------|------|
| biological evolution | mutation | selection |
| neural systems | synaptic growth | pruning |
| artificial intelligence | parameter scaling | model compression |
| industrial systems | infrastructure expansion | efficiency optimization |

Robbie’s Razor remains the governing selection rule:

compression → expression → memory → recursion


The attractor describes the path systems follow while discovering architectures that satisfy this rule.

# 2 Unified Recursion Efficiency Relation

Define:

- **R** — recursive transition rate  
- **E** — available energy per unit time  
- **JCT** — Joules per Coherent Transition  
- **I** — preserved functional information

Recursion efficiency:

S_r = I / JCT

Energetic recursion ceiling:

R ≤ E / JCT


Substitution yields:

R ≤ (E · S_r) / I


Interpretation:

Recursive throughput increases when:

- energy availability **E** increases
- compression efficiency **S_r** improves

Compression-efficient architectures increase **S_r** by preserving more functional structure per unit energetic cost.

# 3 Conservation of Compressed Structure

Stable recursive systems must preserve compressed functional structure across cycles.

Let:

- **I_c** — compressed structure  
- **C_r** — recomputation cost  
- **E** — energetic cost

Loss of compressed structure forces recomputation:

E ∝ C_r


Thus recursive systems must either:

- preserve compressed structure (memory)
- or pay recomputation cost (energy)

This principle underlies:

- Compression–Memory Separation (§11.2)
- Memory–Compute Trade Curve (§11.4.5A)
- Energetic Recursion Ceiling (§11.4.5)
